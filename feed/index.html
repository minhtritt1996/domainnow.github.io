]]> My Son Sanctuary Tourism Attracts International Visitors

(Dân trí) – In 2022, the My Son Sanctuary welcomed nearly 67,000 international visitors, accounting for only 25% of the pre-pandemic figure (2019), but there are signs of recovery in international tourism.

According to Mr. Phan Ho, Head of the My Son Cultural Heritage Management Board (Duy Xuyen District, Quang Nam Province), the two years of the pandemic severely impacted the number of visitors to the My Son Cultural Heritage Site.

The “Legendary Night at My Son” program, held at the site, attracted visitors for nighttime tours.

Despite the prolonged pandemic, efforts were made to attract visitors, including devising strategies to welcome them back when the situation eased. Along with implementing effective pandemic prevention measures to ensure a safe destination, the site was opened to visitors whenever conditions allowed.

In particular, during the challenging period for tourism, the My Son Cultural Heritage Management Board focused on promoting the site to reach out to tourists. This included live-streamed presentations on social media platforms using digital technology.

Foreign tourists return to My Son after the pandemic.

The National Tourism Year 2022 in Quang Nam was an opportunity for My Son to attract visitors. Two programs, “Legendary Night at My Son” and “Apsara Legend,” effectively promoted the image of the My Son Sanctuary to domestic and international tourists.

After the disruption of tourism due to the COVID-19 pandemic, from the second quarter of 2022, as tourism gradually recovered, the number of visitors to the My Son World Cultural Heritage Site began to grow again.

 

By the end of 2022, the number of international visitors steadily increased, with group tours dominating. Notably, the Indian tourist market showed significant growth.

Throughout 2022, the My Son Sanctuary welcomed over 100,000 visitors, including nearly 60,000 international tourists and more than 40,000 domestic visitors, generating revenue of over VND 16.7 billion.

The Apsara dance performed at My Son.

Mr. Phan Ho stated that in 2023, the prospects for visitors to My Son are brighter than in 2022 due to China’s reopening, which will bring back Chinese tourists. “The Chinese market accounts for 25% of the share, so with China’s reopening, we hope to welcome more international tourists to My Son this year,” said Mr. Phan Ho.

In 2023, the My Son Sanctuary is expected to receive 180,000 visitors, including 130,000 international tourists and 50,000 domestic tourists.

Additionally, the My Son Cultural Heritage Management Board will focus on consolidating traditional markets such as Europe, Australia, and the United States, while preparing to welcome tourists from East Asia as these countries reopen. This is a high-demand market for entertainment, so efforts will concentrate on enhancing artistic programs.

]]> Promoting the Value of My Son World Heritage Site in Tandem with Tourism Development

The serene beauty of ancient Cham towers in the My Son World Cultural Heritage. (Photo: TH/Vietnam+)

The My Son Sanctuary, located in Duy Phu commune, Duy Xuyen district, Quang Nam province, is the most famous architectural complex of the Cham people in Vietnam, attracting a large number of domestic and international tourists.

The Cham Pa Legacy in the Heart of Quang

The My Son Sanctuary was built from the late 4th century to the 13th century. In 1898, a Frenchman named M.C. Paris discovered the My Son temple complex hidden in a narrow valley amid dense forests.

Not long after, scientists from the French School of the Far East studied the inscriptions and architectural and sculptural works at My Son.

These researchers unveiled the mystery of My Son, revealing it as the most magnificent and quintessential religious site of the Cham people, continuously constructed over more than 1,000 years.

Initiated in the 4th century by King Bhadravarman (reigned from 349 to 361) and completed in the late 13th or early 14th century under King Jaya Simhavarman III (Che Man), My Son comprises over 70 temples and towers, reflecting various architectural and sculptural styles of different historical periods of the Cham kingdom.

Most architectural works and sculptures at My Son are influenced by Hinduism.

The temples and towers mainly face east, the direction of the rising sun and the abode of deities. However, some towers face west or both east and west, symbolizing the belief in the afterlife of the kings who were deified after their deaths to honor their ancestors.

[Video] My Son Sanctuary: The Cham Pa Legacy in the Heart of Quang

Damaged by war, My Son had only 32 remaining structures by 1975, of which about 20 retained their original form. Unfortunately, the largest structure, Tower A1, which stood 24 meters high and had six auxiliary towers, was considered a masterpiece of Cham architecture but was destroyed by U.S. bombs in late 1969.

Despite many temples and towers being reduced to ruins, My Son remains a unique cultural, artistic, and architectural heritage of humanity.

The main temples in My Son are dedicated to a Linga or depictions of the deity Shiva—the protector of Cham royal lineages. The god worshipped at My Son is Bhadravarman, who founded the first ruling dynasty of the Amaravati region in the 4th century, combined with Shiva’s name, forming the primary deity-king and royal ancestor worship tradition.

On April 29, 1979, the Ministry of Culture and Information issued Decision No. 54VH/QĐ recognizing My Son as an Architectural and Artistic Monument.

On December 1, 1999, the UNESCO World Heritage Committee officially recognized My Son Sanctuary as a World Cultural Heritage.

Promoting Heritage Value Linked to Sustainable Tourism Development

Despite the ups and downs of history, My Son remains a unique cultural, artistic, and architectural treasure of humanity, embodying the intellect and talent of many generations.

Preserving and promoting the value of My Son World Cultural Heritage in conjunction with sustainable tourism development, ensuring harmony between cultural and economic development, and applying modern science and technology in conservation efforts while strictly adhering to regulations on state management, World Heritage conservation conventions, and the Cultural Heritage Law are the guiding principles currently implemented by Duy Xuyen district, Quang Nam province.

According to Phan Ho, Director of the My Son World Cultural Heritage Management Board, significant progress has been made in the management and conservation of My Son over the more than 20 years since its recognition.

The site has collaborated with organizations and individuals both domestically and internationally, such as UNESCO, the Japan International Cooperation Agency (JICA), the Lerici Foundation (Italy), the University of Milan, UNESCO Hanoi Office, the ASI Institute (India), the Institute of Monuments Restoration, the Archaeological Institute, the Department of Heritage, the Department of Culture, Sports and Tourism, and the Quang Nam Center for Heritage Conservation, to restore and enhance the B, C, D, G, K, H, and A tower groups. The remaining groups remain in ruin and have yet to be restored.

The My Son World Cultural Heritage Management Board is implementing new services and products to attract visitors to the site.

From now until 2030, besides the current offerings at the temple complex, the Management Board plans to develop tourism products in the buffer and core zones, including heritage cultural tourism and ecological tourism in the scenic forest area, spanning 1,158 hectares under their management.

The focus will be on developing eco-resort tourism in the buffer zone, centered around Thach Ban Lake and the surrounding primeval forest, positioning My Son as an appealing resort destination.

Starting in 2023, alongside ongoing restoration efforts, the Management Board will strengthen connectivity with destinations along the Central Heritage Journey and major tourism hubs; develop eco-friendly electric vehicle services aligned with modern tourism trends; and improve the quality of human resources, particularly interpreters, to educate visitors on the heritage’s value.

New offerings include renting Cham costumes for photography combined with souvenir production, enhancing image promotion, and advancing research and global promotion of the My Son temple complex’s heritage value.

]]> My Son Sanctuary: Explore the 1600-Year-Old Ruins of the Champa Kingdom

A visit to My Son Sanctuary will add a memorable experience to your trip to Hoi An or Da Nang. This UNESCO World Heritage Site offers a glimpse into the past of the ancient Champa Kingdom, showcasing breathtaking ruins and architectural wonders.

My Son Sanctuary – A UNESCO World Heritage Site
In December 1999, UNESCO recognized My Son Sanctuary as a World Heritage Site. This Cham tower complex is one of Vietnam’s most beautiful and impressive Cham temples. Each temple showcases a unique structural style that reflects the historical stages of the Champa kingdoms. Only about 20 of these temple towers remain at My Son due to damage from natural disasters and war. However, it continues to be a popular destination that attracts tourists from around the world.

1. Where is My Son Sanctuary in Vietnam?

My Son Sanctuary comprises several ancient Cham temples spread across a 2-kilometer-wide valley surrounded by hills and mountains. Though time and war have taken their toll on these relics, what remains of My Son plays an important role in world history and culture.

Location: Duy Phu Commune, Duy Xuyen District, Quang Nam Province
Opening Hours: 6:30 AM – 5:30 PM (every day, including holidays and Tet)
Entrance Fee: 150,000 VND (covers entry to My Son Sanctuary and the Champa Museum). This price applies for self-guided visits and includes a 2-kilometer electric car ride to the center and art performance areas.

1.2. How to Get to My Son Sanctuary?

My Son is approximately 69 kilometers from Da Nang and 45 kilometers from Hoi An Ancient Town. You can reach My Son from either city.

From Hoi An:

  1. By Bike: Take a scooter from Hoi An, follow Tran Hung Dao Street, cross Vinh Dien River, and continue straight to Quang Trung street. Signs will guide you to My Son.
  2. By Bus: Public buses run from Hoi An to My Son, but they do not stop at the entrance. Alternatively, you can reserve a shuttle bus that departs at 8:30 AM and returns at 1:45 PM for around $8.
  3. By Car: Car rentals in Hoi An are limited. If you’re renting a car, expect to travel from Da Nang Airport with a rental costing about $100 for the day.

From Da Nang:

  1. By Bike or Car: Follow National Highway 1 to Nam Phuoc Town (39 km), turn onto Route 537, and follow signs to My Son. Continue on the mountain road for about 5 minutes.
  2. By Bus: Bus number 06 departs from Da Nang every 30 minutes, starting at 5:30 AM until 5 PM, with fares ranging from 8,000 to 30,000 VND.

1.3. Map of My Son Sanctuary

A detailed map is available at the entrance of My Son Sanctuary to help you navigate the site and locate key attractions.

2. History of My Son Sanctuary in Quang Nam

My Son Sanctuary was built between the 4th and 13th centuries CE by the Cham people, a culture that developed along the coast of modern Vietnam and had spiritual origins in Indian Hinduism. The impressive tower temples at My Son served as the political and religious capital of the Champa Kingdom. The stone stele of the Cham royal dynasties confirms My Son’s importance as the sacred heart of the Cham people during those centuries.

3. Highlights of My Son Sanctuary

The sanctuary spans 1158 hectares. It takes about 2 to 3 hours to explore its most notable features.

3.1. Ancient Cham Architecture of My Son Sanctuary

The most captivating aspect of My Son Sanctuary is its unique Cham architecture, heavily influenced by Indian styles. The Cham towers were built on quadrilateral foundations, and each tower has three parts:

  • A sturdy foundation representing the mortal world.
  • A sacred body symbolizing the spiritual world.
  • The tower top, shaped like a man offering flowers, birds, or animals, representing the connection between spirits and humans.

Though many structures have been damaged, the remaining towers and sculptures offer a glimpse into the rich cultural heritage of the Cham people.

3.2. My Son Sanctuary Museum

After entering the sanctuary, visitors should first explore the museum. The museum houses exhibits on My Son’s relics and history, providing valuable context for understanding the site’s significance. Be aware that the museum closes half an hour earlier than the sanctuary.

3.3. Inspiring Apsara Dance in My Son Sanctuary

In addition to its architectural beauty, My Son offers daily performances of traditional Cham dances. Local dancers perform a variety of dances, from the iconic Shiva and Apsara dances to joyful fan and water dances. These performances, accompanied by folk music, enhance the sacred atmosphere of the sanctuary.

3.4. Kate Festival in My Son Sanctuary

Every October, the Cham people celebrate the Kate festival, or Mbang Kate, for three days. This event is the Cham people’s most significant celebration, honoring their heroes and ancestors. The festival features vibrant traditional costumes, passionate dances, and symphonic music, creating a surreal cultural experience.

4. Must-Try Food Near My Son Sanctuary

While visiting My Son, be sure to try some of Central Vietnam’s most iconic dishes, such as:

  • Quang Noodles (Mi Quang)
  • Cau Mong Smoked Calf (Be Thui Cau Mong)
  • Phu Chiem Noodles (Mi Phu Chiem)
  • Vietnamese Pancakes (Banh Xeo)
  • Rice Crackers (Banh Trang Dap)

There are a few restaurants at the entrance to My Son and eateries in nearby towns where you can sample these delicacies.

5. Where to Stay for Your Visit to My Son Sanctuary?

Accommodation options near My Son Sanctuary are limited, so visitors are advised to stay in Hoi An or Da Nang. Hotels and resorts in these areas offer convenient access to My Son and other attractions.

For a perfect family vacation with cultural experiences, consider staying at Vinpearl Resort & Golf Nam Hoi An, located on Binh Minh beach, just a 20-minute drive from Hoi An. Guests can also enjoy the nearby amusement park, VinWonders Nam Hoi An.

My Son Sanctuary remains a key destination for history lovers and those seeking to immerse themselves in the Cham culture of Vietnam.

]]> The Mỹ Sơn Sanctuary, recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site for 20 years, has experienced a significant increase in visitor numbers, reaching 420,000 annually. However, despite its historical and cultural importance, the site has yet to fully capitalize on its potential to generate lasting benefits for the local community. Although the sanctuary is a beautiful and historically significant location, it struggles to retain visitors for extended periods due to the lack of additional services and attractions that could enhance the overall tourist experience.

Tourists, particularly those from Europe, Japan, and India, who make up a significant portion of visitors, have expressed their disappointment regarding the lack of local products, shopping options, and cultural experiences beyond the temple complex itself. The visitors often leave the site shortly after their temple tour, as there are few opportunities for them to engage more deeply with the region’s culture or enjoy leisure activities.

The sanctuary is visually striking, surrounded by lush nature and mountains, and the temple complex itself remains relatively well-preserved. However, the overall tourist experience is monotonous and does not include activities or services that might encourage guests to stay longer. Visitors are typically transported by electric vehicles from the entrance to the temple complex, where they explore the site on foot. The journey usually lasts less than an hour, leaving little to stimulate further exploration or encourage tourists to spend more time or money in the area.

Despite the rising number of visitors, the local community has not seen significant benefits from the influx of tourists. The site remains a “stopover” rather than a fully developed destination. The surrounding community has not been effectively integrated into the tourism ecosystem, meaning that many local residents do not benefit from tourism-related services. There are very few businesses offering the kinds of services that could enhance a tourist’s visit, such as shopping or cultural experiences. As a result, tourists often return to nearby cities like Hội An or Da Nang, where the tourism infrastructure is more developed.

There have been some initiatives to address this issue, such as the establishment of a community tourism village in 2013, which was supported by the International Labour Organization (ILO). The village was intended to offer accommodation and other services, managed by local families. However, this project has largely failed, with low occupancy rates and many of the lodgings remaining closed. The residents, who were initially encouraged to shift from farming to tourism-related activities, still struggle to benefit economically from their proximity to the sanctuary.

Additionally, there is only one hotel near the sanctuary, which has struggled to attract guests despite significant investment. This lack of services around the sanctuary, such as cultural attractions, local products, and diverse dining options, has limited the potential for tourism to create a sustainable economic benefit for the surrounding community.

In contrast to other heritage sites like Hội An, which have successfully created a thriving tourism ecosystem that benefits local businesses and provides a wide range of services for visitors, Mỹ Sơn remains underdeveloped in terms of tourist infrastructure. While it continues to attract significant numbers of visitors, without more comprehensive development and the creation of a broader tourism ecosystem, Mỹ Sơn is unlikely to reach its full potential as a key tourism destination.

Nestled within a secluded valley surrounded by majestic mountains, this site is a treasure trove of historical, cultural, and architectural significance.

Mỹ Sơn’s architecture is notable for its intricacy and symbolism. The structures were crafted from brick and stone without the use of mortar, showcasing remarkable engineering skills. Despite the ravages of time and war, Mỹ Sơn retains its spiritual essence and historical value. Conservation efforts have been underway to restore and preserve the site, ensuring its legacy endures for future generations. Mỹ Sơn not only captivates visitors with its mysterious allure but also serves as a testament to the cultural and artistic prowess of the Champa civilization.

As a globally significant heritage site, Mỹ Sơn stands as a symbol of Vietnam’s rich and diverse cultural history, attracting scholars, historians, and tourists from all over the world.

]]> Hoi An, a charming town in central Vietnam, is not only renowned for its ancient architecture and cultural heritage but also its vibrant and diverse culinary scene. The local cuisine of Hoi An is a delightful fusion of Vietnamese, Chinese, and Japanese influences, resulting in a unique and mouthwatering array of dishes. In this article, Hoi An Village Tours will explore the top most popular dishes in Hoi An, allowing you to embark on a gastronomic journey through the flavors and aromas of this enchanting town.

Cao Lau:
Cao Lau is a signature dish of Hoi An and a must-try for any visitor. This tantalizing bowl of thick rice noodles is topped with slices of succulent pork, fragrant herbs, and crunchy bean sprouts. The secret lies in the special water used to cook the noodles, made from a local well that supposedly possesses unique properties. The combination of textures and flavors makes Cao Lau a true culinary delight.

White Rose Dumplings (Banh Bao Vac):
White Rose Dumplings, or Banh Bao Vac, are delicate and exquisite dumplings shaped like white roses. Made from translucent rice flour wrappers, these dumplings are filled with a mixture of shrimp and fragrant herbs. Served with a tangy dip, these bite-sized delights are not only visually appealing but also bursting with flavor.

Banh Mi:
Vietnam’s famous sandwich, Banh Mi, has also found its way into the hearts and stomachs of Hoi An’s locals and visitors. A crusty baguette is filled with a variety of fillings, including grilled pork, pate, pickled vegetables, cilantro, and chili sauce. The combination of savory, sweet, and tangy flavors creates a culinary symphony that will leave you craving for more.

Mi Quang:
Mi Quang is a traditional noodle dish that originated in the Quang Nam province, where Hoi An is located. This dish features thick rice noodles, topped with a mix of marinated pork, shrimp, fresh herbs, peanuts, and crispy rice crackers. The broth is infused with turmeric, giving it a vibrant yellow color and a distinct flavor. Mi Quang is a hearty and satisfying dish that will warm your soul.

Com Ga (Chicken Rice):
Com Ga, or Chicken Rice, is a simple yet flavorful dish that Hoi An is known for. Tender poached chicken is served on a bed of fragrant rice cooked in chicken broth, accompanied by a side of fresh herbs, pickles, and chili sauce. The combination of tender meat, aromatic rice, and zesty flavors makes Com Ga a true comfort food.

Banh Xeo:
Banh Xeo is a Vietnamese-style pancake that is popular throughout the country, including Hoi An. A crispy rice flour pancake is filled with a mixture of shrimp, pork, bean sprouts, and green onions. Wrap it in fresh lettuce leaves, add herbs, and dip it in a tangy sauce for an explosion of flavors and textures.

Banh Beo:
Banh Beo are small, steamed rice cakes served in tiny dishes. Topped with dried shrimp, scallion oil, and crispy pork rinds, these bite-sized treats are savory and satisfying. Dip them in a tangy fish sauce for an added burst of flavor. Banh Beo is a popular street food snack that is perfect for sharing with friends.

Banh Dap:
Banh Dap is a unique dish that showcases the creativity of Hoi An’s culinary scene. It consists of two layers of rice crackers, one soft and one crispy, topped with a variety of ingredients such as ground pork, herbs, and peanuts. The contrasting textures and flavors make Banh Dap a delightful and playful dish to enjoy.

Banh Can:
Banh Can are small round pancakes made from rice flour and cooked on a griddle. They are typically filled with quail eggs, shrimp, and green onions, resulting in a savory and satisfying treat. Dip them in a flavorful sauce for an extra kick of taste.

Che:
Finish your culinary journey in Hoi An with a bowl of Che, a traditional Vietnamese dessert. This sweet soup-like dessert is made with a variety of ingredients such as beans, jelly, fruits, and coconut milk. Whether it’s Che Ba Ba, Che Dau Xanh, or Che Bap, the colorful and refreshing flavors of Che will leave a lasting impression.

Conclusion:
Hoi An’s culinary scene is a treasure trove of flavors and aromas, offering a delightful blend of traditional and innovative dishes. From the iconic Cao Lau to the delicate White Rose Dumplings and the beloved Banh Mi, the top most popular dishes in Hoi An will take you on a gastronomic adventure like no other. Indulge in these mouthwatering delights, and let the vibrant culinary culture of Hoi An leave a lasting impression on your taste buds.
